Early applications of computing were concerned mainly with numeric computation: evaluating mathematical functions, solving equations, or performing scientific and statistical calculations. Subsequently applications became increasingly concerned with processing text and not just numbers. Even so, many applications—not just in science and engineering—still manipulate numbers. Hence it pays to have a variety of tools for performing standard numeric computations. Some of these tools can solve problems that are difficult or impossible to solve by mathematical analysis alone; others can solve problems that ordinarily require more sophisticated mathematical techniques than non-experts may possess.

It would be wrong to conclude that computing is a replacement for mathematics. Rather, computing complements mathematics: it helps put mathematics into practice, and mathematics helps in performing computation.

Approximation and simulation provide two means for finding "good enough" solutions to numeric problems. For example, it is impossible to compute an exact value of π, the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter, because π's decimal representation contains infinitely many digits. Still, one can approximate π by generating as many of these digits as desired. For another example, it may be hard to compute the launch velocity for a satellite and expensive to determine it by experimentation, but the effects of different launch velocities can be determined by simulation.
