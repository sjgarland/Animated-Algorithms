Exact answers to questions are the most satisfying, provided we can get them. But often exact answers are impossible or extremely difficult to obtain, and we may be willing to settle for approximate answers instead. The fact that we cannot write down exact decimal representations for irrational numbers such as $pi$ causes us little difficulty: for most purposes it is enough to know that $pi$ is approximately 3.14159265. And to win a prize for counting the number of jelly beans in a large jar, we need only estimate that number more accurately than our competitors.

Approximation, then, helps us obtain answers that are good enough for the need at hand. To illustrate the general nature of approximations, we develop several algorithms for approximating zeroes of functions. Exercises suggest some algorithms for approximating other interesting values.

Zeroes of functions

Many problems can be solved by finding a zero of a function f, that is, by finding a value x such that f(x)\|\|=\|\|0. In Chapter 4, we found the effective interest rate for a loan by finding a zero of a function f such that f(r) is the final balance of the loan when the interest rate is r. For another example, to find the square root of 2, we can define a function f by letting f(x)\ = $x sup 2 ~-~ 2$ and then find a zero of f.

More generally, we can reduce the problem of computing the inverse of a one-to-one function to the problem of computing zeroes of related functions. If g is a one-to-one function (i.e., if g(x)\|\|=\|\|g(y) implies x\|\|=\|\|y), then the inverse of g is that function $g sup -1$ such that .BS $g sup -1$(y) = x if and only if g(x)\|\|=\|\|y .BE (notice that g must be one-to-one for $g sup -1$ to be a function). Now computing $g sup -1$(y) is equivalent to finding a zero of the function $g sub y$, where $g sub y (x)$\ = $g(x) ~-~ y$. Since the square root function is the inverse of the square function, computing square roots is just a special case of computing inverses of functions.

For yet another example, students who have studied calculus can reduce the problem of finding the maximum or minimum value of a function to the problem of finding the zeroes of the derivative of that function. Thus one technique, namely finding the zeroes of functions, helps solve many seemingly disparate problems.
